The Origins of Gutenborg

with Drake Preston, OIP Production Designer

As with most projects, I started designing with words—mind mapping associations and juxtapositions of language. I asked myself questions like: what’s already been done in publishing? What are publishers doing right now? On an indie scale? National? Looking through these lenses creates a firm foundation to start tethering ideas into concepts.

Initially, I explored monograms for OIP—pulling from literary symbols as inspiration (i.e. “the pilcrow”).

However, I consistently found myself being drawn to icons or “mascots” (e.g. Penguin, The Bodley Head, Chatto & Windus). An icon can be a versatile element in any brand toolkit, especially as a publisher as it works great at small scales (e.g. the spine of a book).

I thought back to early conversations with Eliza about the heart, soul, and vision for OIP. Inspirations like: History. Technology. Books. Stamps. Lead type. Motorcycles. Sex Pistols.

One thing that always stuck with me was this idea of the juxtaposition of old and new. Stepping into the future with a respect for what came before. An electric engine inside of a 1965 Ford Mustang Shelby GT350. I started researching Johannes Gutenberg, the inventor of letterpress printing, and I wondered how I might marry this history with something representative of OIP’s focus on the future.

Thus, Gutenborg was born. The amalgamation of a 1969 Triumph Trident headlight, a well-preserved printing plate circa 1443, and a self-healing, energy-generating wheel. A fun detail: the position of the headlamp was intentionally shifted to appear “chin up”—embodying a look of hope, courage, and possibility.
